Section 1: Initial and Final Steady States

This section formalizes the economy before the shock (Period 0) and after labor has fully reallocated to the new technology (Period 2).

Initial Steady State (Period 0)

The economy begins in a steady state with full labor utilization and wages equalized across sectors. Sectoral labor shares exactly match the household preference weights: \( N_{A,0} = \phi \) and \( N_{B,0} = 1 - \phi \). All nominal prices and wages are normalized to unity:

$$ W_{A,0} = W_{B,0} = P_{A,0} = P_{B,0} = P_0 = 1 $$

At these normalized prices, the steady-state sector outputs simply map to their labor shares (\( Y_{A,0} = \phi, Y_{B,0} = 1-\phi \)). Aggregate output and real consumption are therefore equal to unity:

$$ Y_0 = C_0 = 1 $$

Final Steady State (Period 2)

At the beginning of Period 1, Sector A experiences a permanent positive technology shock from \( Z_0 \) to \( Z \). By Period 2, workers have fully reallocated across sectors to re-equilibrate wages:

$$ W_{A,2} = W_{B,2} = P_{B,2} = 1 $$

If the new technology is sufficiently labor-displacing (substitutability in production \( \eta \) exceeds the elasticity of demand \( \epsilon \)), Sector A experiences a collapse in its relative price that dominates the pure productivity gain. As a result, the economy undergoes a structural transformation where labor permanently leaves Sector A:

$$ N_{A,2} < N_{A,0} \iff \eta> \epsilon $$

Despite the labor displacement in Sector A, aggregate limits expand due to the technological gain (\( Z > Z_0 \)). This permanent shift in frontier production translates directly into an expanded steady-state output and consumption limit:

$$ Y_2 = C_2 > Y_0 $$

All technological rents generated by the new technology \( Z \) are assumed to be disbursed evenly among all households in this final steady state, ensuring equal consumption across constrained and unconstrained agents (\( C_{c,2} = C_{u,2} = C_2 \)).

Section 2: Period 1 Equilibrium & Phase-Space

This document formalizes the Period 1 equilibrium conditions and assumptions as implemented in the numerical phase space solver for the "Technological Recessions" model.

1. Production and Technology

The economy consists of two sectors: Sector A (exposed to the technology shock) and Sector B (unexposed). The size of Sector A is denoted by \( \phi \).

Sector B (Unexposed)

Sector B operates with a linear production technology using labor \( N_B \):

$$ Y_B = N_B $$

Assuming perfect competition, Sector B nominal wages are rigid downward relative to the steady state (\( W_B = 1 \)). The price of Good B is determined by market clearing up to capacity (\( 1 - \phi \)):

$$ P_B = \max(1, \text{Market Clearing Price}) \ge W_B = 1 $$

Sector A (Exposed)

Sector A produces using a CES production function combining a fixed sector-specific factor \( Z \) and labor \( N_A \), with an elasticity of substitution \( \eta \):

$$ Y_A(N_A) = A \left[ \gamma^{\frac{1}{\eta}} Z^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}} + (1-\gamma)^{\frac{1}{\eta}} N_A^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}} \right]^{\frac{\eta}{\eta-1}} $$

where \( \gamma \) is a distribution parameter (calibrated to \( 0.3 \)) and the exogenous scaling factors \( A \) and \( Z \) ensure consistency with the steady state.

We assume Downward Nominal Wage Rigidity (DNWR) across the economy, such that the wage in Sector A cannot fall below 1 (\( W_A = 1 \)). Firms in Sector A hire labor up to the point where the marginal product of labor equals the real product wage:

$$ P_A \cdot MPL_A(N_A) = W_A = 1 \implies P_A = \frac{1}{MPL_A(N_A)} $$

2. Demand and Prices

Households have CES preferences over the two goods with an elasticity of substitution \( \epsilon \):

$$ C = \left[ \phi^{\frac{1}{\epsilon}} C_A^{\frac{\epsilon-1}{\epsilon}} + (1-\phi)^{\frac{1}{\epsilon}} C_B^{\frac{\epsilon-1}{\epsilon}} \right]^{\frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon-1}} $$

This yields the standard aggregate price index \( P_1 \):

$$ P_1 = \left[ \phi P_A^{1-\epsilon} + (1-\phi) P_B^{1-\epsilon} \right]^{\frac{1}{1-\epsilon}} = \left[ \phi P_A^{1-\epsilon} + (1-\phi) \right]^{\frac{1}{1-\epsilon}} $$

3. Heterogeneous Households and Consumption

The economy is populated by a continuum of agents. In the exposed sector, a fraction \( \mu \) of agents are "constrained" (hand-to-mouth), meaning they cannot borrow or save and simply consume their current real labor income. The remaining agents in both sectors are "unconstrained" (Ricardian) and follow the standard Euler equation.

The fraction of constrained agents in the total economy is therefore \( \varphi = \phi \mu \).

Unconstrained Agents

Unconstrained agents optimize consumption intertemporally, yielding the standard Euler equation:

$$ C_u = C_2 \left[ \beta (1+i) \frac{P_1}{P_2} \right]^{-\sigma} $$

where \( C_2 \) and \( P_2 \) are the Period 2 (steady state) consumption and price index, \( \beta \) is the discount factor, \( i \) is the nominal interest rate set by monetary policy, and \( \sigma \) is the intertemporal elasticity of substitution.

Constrained Agents

Constrained agents in Sector A earn the nominal wage \( W_A = 1 \) and face unemployment risk if \( N_A < \phi \). Assuming uniform rationing of hours among exposed workers, their real consumption is:

$$ C_c = \frac{W_A (N_A / \phi)}{P_1} = \frac{N_A}{\phi P_1} $$

*(At Full Employment, \( N_A = \phi \), so \( C_c = 1 / P_1 \))*.

Market Clearing

Total aggregate consumption \( C \) is the weighted average of constrained and unconstrained consumption. Since constrained agents make up a share \( \varphi = \phi \mu \) of the total economy:

$$ C = (\phi \mu) C_c + (1 - \phi \mu) C_u $$

Solving this for \( \mu \), we get the critical equation used by the numerical solver to trace the phase boundaries:

$$ \mu = \frac{C_u - C}{\phi (C_u - C_c)} $$

4. Phase Regimes

The boundaries of the phase space are derived by solving the Period 1 equilibrium under active constraints:

  1. Full Employment: The economy is unconstrained (\( N_A = \phi, N_B = 1-\phi \)). \( Y_A = Y_A(\phi) \), \( C_c = 1/P_1 \). This holds as long as \( \mu \leq \mu_{gap} \).
  2. Technological Unemployment: \( N_A < \phi \), but \( N_B=1-\phi \). Sector A sheds labor, but nominal rigidity prevents Sector B from absorbing them since demand isn't high enough. Holds for \( \mu_{gap} < \mu \leq \mu_{contagion} \).
  3. Keynesian Unemployment: \( N_A < \phi \) and \( N_B < 1-\phi \). Deficient aggregate demand spreads to the unexposed sector. Holds for \( \mu_{contagion} < \mu \leq \mu_{recession} \).
  4. Technological Recession: The shock is so severe that it hits the zero lower bound on Sector A prices (\( P_A \to 0 \)) resulting from downward nominal wage rigidity (\( W_A = 1 \)), causing maximum macroeconomic contraction where output drops across the board globally. Holds when \( \mu > \mu_{recession} \).

Section 3: Extensions & Policy Controls

The "Technological Recessions" model features four structural extensions that dictate how the economy absorbs the labor-displacing shock. Adjusting these parameters reveals how different policy levers can mitigate or exacerbate the recessionary regions.

1. Unequal Employment Rationing (\( \theta \))

Imperfect Income Risk-Sharing. In the baseline, labor rationing is distributed equally so all constrained workers share the income loss identically. \( \theta \) represents the degree of income sharing between the employed and unemployed. When labor demand falls (\( N_{A,1} < \phi \)), the employed earn the rigid wage \( W_{A,1}=1 \) but pay a tax \( \tau=\theta \frac{\phi - N_{A,1}}{\phi} \) to fund the unemployed. The net nominal incomes are:

$$ I^e(\theta) = 1 - \theta \frac{\phi - N_{A,1}}{\phi} \qquad\text{and}\qquad I^u(\theta) = \theta \frac{N_{A,1}}{\phi} $$

When \( \theta=1 \), \( I^e = I^u = N_{A,1}/\phi \) (the baseline equal-rationing case). As \( \theta \to 0 \), the unemployed receive nothing (\( I^u \to 0 \)), generating severe, asymmetric, idiosyncratic income risk which forces a more dramatic aggregate contraction in demand through the Euler equation and precautionary savings (though precautionary savings are turned off in this simple formulation, the mean-preserving spread affects welfare).

2. Shared Technological Ownership (\( \chi \))

Universal Basic Income / AI Dividend. By default, firm profits only flow to unconstrained agents. \( \chi \in [0, 1] \) represents the fraction of Sector A rents redistributed directly back to constrained agents (who hand-to-mouth consume all of it). The nominal profits are:

$$ \Pi_{A,1} = P_{A,1} Y_{A,1} - W_{A,1} N_{A,1} $$

The total income of the constrained agents in Sector A is now bolstered by these profit dividends, offsetting the mechanical collapse in \( N_{A,1} \):

$$ C_{c,1} = \frac{1}{\phi}\frac{W_{A,1} N_{A,1} + \chi \Pi_{A,1}}{P_1} $$

3. Labor Mobility (\( \zeta \))

Short-Term Reallocation. In the baseline, displaced workers cannot switch sectors until Period 2. \( \zeta \in [0,1] \) allows a fraction of the newly displaced Sector A pool (\( \phi - N_{A,1} \)) to successfully retrain and migrate to Sector B in Period 1. The labor capacity of Sector B bounds upward:

$$ Y_{B,1}^* = 1 - \phi + \zeta(\phi - N_{A,1}) $$

Retrained workers join the unconstrained cohort. The fraction of strictly constrained agents shrinks from \( \varphi = \phi\mu \) down to \( \varphi_{eff} = \phi\mu(1-\zeta) \). Aggregate demand becomes a weighted function of the newly expanded Ricardian pool:

$$ C_1 = \mu \frac{N_{A,1}}{P_1} + \left[ 1 - \varphi(1 - \zeta) \right] C_{u,1} $$

4. Technology Inhibition (\( \alpha \))

Gradual Deployment. \( \alpha \in (0, 1] \) suppresses the immediate efficiency of the new technology, acting as a phased-rollout or containment policy. Only a fraction of the productivity jump \( Z \) is active in Period 1, while the full \( Z \) unlocks in Period 2. The Period 1 production frontier is:

$$ Y_{A,1}(\alpha) = A \left[ \gamma^{\frac{1}{\eta}} (\alpha Z)^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}} + (1-\gamma)^{\frac{1}{\eta}} N_{A,1}^{\frac{\eta-1}{\eta}} \right]^{\frac{\eta}{\eta-1}} $$

Because \( \alpha Z < Z \), the relative price \( P_A \) does not collapse as violently, effectively shielding the Marginal Product of Labor (\( MPL_A \)) from falling below the rigid wage boundary of 1, thereby preserving employment \( N_{A,1} \) at the cost of aggregate output \( Y_1 \).
